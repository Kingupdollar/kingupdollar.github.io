Lesson 1

The Structure of An Eyeball

Learning objective: Explain the optical principles of the eye

Skills:

  • Identify the different parts of an eyeball
  • Identify the order that the light stimuli pass through each part of the eyeball

The front visible part of the eye is made up of the whitish sclera, a colored iris, and the pupil. The outermost layer of an eyeball, known as the fibrous tunic, is composed of the cornea and sclera, which provide shape to the eye and support the deeper structures.

The eye includes a lens similar to lenses found in optical instruments such as cameras and the same physics principles can be applied. The pupil of the human eye is its aperture. Refraction in the cornea causes the effective aperture (the entrance pupil) to differ slightly from the physical pupil diameter.

The retina is the innermost, light-sensitive layer of tissue of the eye. If we continue using a camera as a metaphor of an eyeball, the retina serves as the similar function as a film in the camera to sense the light and transmit the stimuli to the brain. To be more specific, the optics of the eye create a focused two-dimensional image of the visual world on the retina, which translates that image into electrical neural impulses to the brain to create visual perception.
